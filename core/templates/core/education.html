What is Medical Maggot Therapy?

Also called Larval Debridement Therapy (LDT)

Medical maggot therapy uses sterile fly larvae to remove dead tissue from chronic wounds. The larvae selectively digest necrotic tissue while preserving healthy tissue.

From Laboratory to Patient

The Clinical Lifecycle of Medical Maggot Therapy

1
Laboratory Cultivation

Specialized flies are bred under controlled conditions to produce medical-grade larvae.

2
Sterilization Process

Eggs undergo strict sterilization to ensure they are free of bacteria and safe for use.

3
Medical Packaging

Sterile larvae are packaged in containers designed to preserve viability during transit.

4
Cold Storage

Larvae are stored in temperature-controlled environments to maintain viability.

5
Clinical Acquisition

Healthcare institutions obtain sterile larvae through regulated supply chains.

6
Patient Wound Therapy

Larvae remove dead tissue, disinfect the wound, and stimulate natural healing.

Clinical Benefits

  • Selective removal of necrotic tissue
  • Effective against antibiotic-resistant bacteria
  • Reduced need for surgical debridement
  • Faster wound healing
  • Cost-effective treatment
